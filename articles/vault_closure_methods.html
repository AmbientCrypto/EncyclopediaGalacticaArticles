3.1 Boltwork Architecture
At the heart of any vault door lies the boltwork, the robust assembly responsible for physically binding the door to its frame, distributing immense loads, and resisting deformation under attack. Conversely, linear bolt systems employ multiple horizontal or vertical bolts sliding into sturdy receivers, offering simplicity and reliability, particularly effective in high-security safes and smaller vaults where uniform stress distribution across the frame is paramount. The sheer scale is staggering; a major bank vault door might feature dozens of bolts, each several inches thick and made from hardened steel alloys, collectively capable of resisting many tons of force. 3.2 Lock Typologies and Mechanisms
While boltwork forms the physical barrier, the lock is the brain controlling its engagement. Two primary mechanical lock types dominate high-security vault applications: the pin-tumbler and the disc-detainer. Pin-tumbler locks, popularized by Linus Yale Jr. and ubiquitous in key-operated systems, rely on aligning a series of driver pins and key pins at the shear line within a cylindrical plug. Only the correct key lifts each pin pair precisely to this line, allowing the plug to rotate and retract the bolt. Their vulnerability lies in susceptibility to picking, bumping, and decoding if manufacturing tolerances aren’t extremely tight. Disc-detainer locks, championed by designers like Emil Henriksson and later refined by Abloy, utilize rotating discs with precisely cut gates. The correct key rotates each disc to align its gate, forming a pathway for a sidebar or fence to drop, enabling bolt retraction. Disc-detainers generally offer superior pick resistance due to their rotating action and the need for precise simultaneous alignment. Combination locks, whether mechanical or electronic dials, remove the key vulnerability entirely. Mechanical variants utilize a wheel pack â€“ a series of discs or tumblers mounted on a spindle. Each disc has a gate; rotating the dial aligns a drive pin with each gate in sequence. Only the correct sequence of rotations brings all gates into alignment simultaneously, allowing a fence to drop and release the bolt mechanism. High-security examples, like those from Sargent & Greenleaf, feature intricate false gates on the wheels to frustrate manipulation attempts. Key control is paramount, extending beyond the lock mechanism itself to the physical key. The 19th century laid the indispensable foundations of modern bank vault security, transforming storage from robust furniture into formidable architecture. The bank robbery epidemics of the 1960s-1980s, particularly in the US, spurred further innovation in locking. Magnetic locks, utilizing powerful electromagnets sealed within the door structure that engaged massive armatures in the frame, offered incredible holding force and could be integrated with rapid-release panic systems. Time locks evolved from complex clockwork mechanisms to precise electronic timers, ensuring vaults remained sealed outside business hours, even if combinations were compromised under duress. The focus also expanded beyond the door itself to include walls, floors, and ceilings, with reinforced concrete becoming the standard monolith encasing the vault chamber, designed to resist tunnelling and shaped charges.
