The fundamental principle enabling the network to pinpoint the origin of a gravitational wave signal is triangulation via time-of-arrival differences. By meticulously measuring these tiny time delays (differential arrival times) between the signals recorded at each site, scientists can constrain the direction the waves came from. Combining the signal amplitudes and phases measured by multiple detectors with distinct orientations provides additional information about the source’s location in the sky and the orientation of its orbital plane relative to Earth, further refining the localization and characterization of the source. This triangulation capability transforms the network from a collection of sensitive microphones into a directional antenna, capable of pointing back towards the cosmic sirens.

Beyond Ground: Space-Based Detection

The triumphant symphony of detections from the global network of LIGO, Virgo, and KAGRA, chronicled in the previous section, has irrevocably confirmed gravitational waves as a fundamental cosmic messenger. Yet, for all their extraordinary sensitivity to the violent mergers of stellar-mass black holes and neutron stars in the audio band, these terrestrial observatories are fundamentally constrained. Earth itself imposes insurmountable limitations: seismic noise masks frequencies below about 10 Hz, while the practical size of vacuum tubes caps the arm length, limiting sensitivity to low-frequency waves requiring baselines far exceeding kilometers. Furthermore, the gravitational rumble of human activity and the constant vibrations of our planet create a noisy floor obscuring the subtler, longer-period ripples permeating the cosmos. But the gravitational wave spectrum extends far beyond the reach of ground-based ears. To listen to the slower, grander movements of truly massive objects ‚Äì the mergers of galaxy-spanning black holes and the intricate dances within galactic cores ‚Äì demands escaping Earth’s gravity well and its attendant noise. This necessity propels us into the next frontier: space-based gravitational wave astronomy, where the Laser Interferometer Space Antenna (LISA) promises to open an entirely new chapter in our exploration of the universe.

The LISA Concept: A Giant Interferometer in Space
Conceived decades ago as a visionary successor to ground-based detectors, LISA embodies an audacious engineering feat on an interplanetary scale. Unlike its terrestrial counterparts confined to fixed locations, LISA will be a constellation of three identical spacecraft, forming a vast, nearly equilateral triangle in space. Each spacecraft will trail Earth in a heliocentric orbit, approximately 50 million kilometers behind our planet, maintaining an arm length of a staggering 2.5 million kilometers ‚Äì roughly six times the Earth-Moon distance. This colossal baseline is the key to accessing the millihertz frequency band (0.1 mHz to 100 mHz), a realm inaccessible from the ground. At the heart of each spacecraft are freely floating test masses, cubes made of a gold-platinum alloy, shielded from all non-gravitational forces ‚Äì solar radiation pressure, solar wind, even the minuscule pressure from cosmic rays. These test masses define the endpoints of the interferometer arms; they are the mirrors in free fall, responding only to the curvature of spacetime itself, pure embodiments of Einstein’s geodesic motion. The spacecraft, acting as sophisticated protective cages, actively shield the test masses from external disturbances using micro-newton thrusters, constantly adjusting their position to precisely follow the masses without ever touching them ‚Äì a concept known as “drag-free flight.” Laser beams, exchanged between the spacecraft with astonishing power and stability, measure the minute changes in the distance between these free-falling test masses across the millions of kilometers of void. Crucially, because the arms are so long, LISA employs a technique called “Time-Delay Interferometry” (TDI). This complex data processing method mathematically combines the laser phase measurements taken at different times to synthesize virtual interferometers with equal arm lengths, effectively canceling out the overwhelming laser frequency noise caused by slight jitters in the master lasers ‚Äì noise that would otherwise swamp the minuscule gravitational wave signal. The result is a sensitivity capable of measuring distance changes, or strains, on the order of picometers (10^-12 meters) over gigameter baselines, translating to a strain sensitivity of about 10^-20 to 10^-23 in the millihertz band. It is, in essence, a Michelson interferometer writ large across the inner Solar System, its laser beams stitching together a web of precision spanning millions of kilometers to sense the subtlest tremors in spacetime.

Science Goals: Probing the Massive and the Ancient
Operating in the serene quiet of space and tuned to millihertz frequencies, LISA will hear a cosmic chorus utterly distinct from the violent chirps detected by LIGO and Virgo. Its primary targets are the mergers of supermassive black holes (SMBHs), behemoths ranging from hundreds of thousands to tens of billions of solar masses residing at the centers of galaxies. As galaxies collide throughout cosmic history, their central SMBHs sink towards each other, eventually forming binaries. LISA will detect these binaries years, decades, or even centuries before their final coalescence, tracking their slow inspiral with exquisite precision. This long observation window is revolutionary. For a binary like 3C 66B ‚Äì a candidate SMBH binary system ‚Äì LISA could measure its orbital parameters, masses, spins, and distance with unprecedented accuracy months or years before merger, providing a detailed prediction of the merger time and location for electromagnetic observatories. Witnessing the crescendo of such a merger, a process radiating gravitational waves equivalent to the combined light of all stars in the observable universe for millions of years compressed into minutes or hours, will offer a direct probe into how galaxies assemble and their central engines evolve. Beyond these titanic mergers, LISA will excel at detecting Extreme Mass Ratio Inspirals (EMRIs). These occur when a stellar-mass compact object ‚Äì a stellar black hole or neutron star ‚Äì is captured into a close, relativistic orbit around a central SMBH. Over months or years, the smaller object spirals inward due to gravitational wave emission before being finally swallowed. The complex, precessing orbit acts like a probe, mapping the spacetime geometry around the SMBH with incredible fidelity. An EMRI plunging into Sagittarius A*, the 4-million-solar-mass black hole at our galaxy’s center, would allow LISA to test General Relativity in the strong-field regime around a spinning Kerr black hole with parameters measured to better than one part in a thousand, potentially revealing deviations from Einstein’s predictions. Furthermore, LISA will survey the population of millions of ultra-compact binary systems within our own Milky Way galaxy ‚Äì pairs of white dwarfs, neutron stars, or stellar black holes in tight orbits emitting continuous gravitational waves. This galactic foreground will be a rich source for understanding stellar evolution endpoints and binary dynamics. Perhaps most profoundly, LISA holds the potential to detect a stochastic gravitational wave background generated in the very early universe, potentially from processes like cosmological phase transitions or cosmic strings, offering a unique glimpse into physics at energy scales far beyond the reach of particle accelerators. The mission’s predecessor, LISA Pathfinder, launched in 2015, was a resounding success. It demonstrated drag-free control and gravitational sensing at a level more than five times better than required for LISA, proving the core technology of free-falling test masses and picometer-level laser metrology in space. Pathfinder’s silent, ultra-stable environment confirmed that the millihertz band is indeed a quiet oasis, perfect for listening to the universe’s deepest hums and rumbles.

Technological Marvels and Challenges
Realizing the LISA vision demands overcoming a constellation of formidable technological hurdles, pushing the boundaries of precision measurement, spacecraft control, and long-duration space operations. The drag-free control system is arguably the most critical. Each spacecraft must continuously adjust its position, using micro-newton colloidal thrusters or similar technology, to precisely follow the free-falling test mass housed within it, shielding it from external forces to better than a femto-newton level. This requires ultra-precile gravitational reference sensors surrounding the test mass, capable of sensing its position relative to the spacecraft with nanometer accuracy using capacitive sensing, while generating no disturbing forces. Any residual force noise would directly masquerade as a gravitational wave signal. The laser metrology system is equally astounding. LISA requires lasers with unprecedented frequency stability ‚Äì fluctuations smaller than one part in 10^13 per square root Hertz over the mission lifetime. Semiconductor lasers amplified to several watts, stabilized against ultra-stable optical cavities or molecular references, will be employed. The laser beams, sent across 2.5 million kilometers, diverge to spots kilometers wide by the time they reach the distant spacecraft. Capturing enough photons for a usable signal requires telescopes about 30 cm in diameter on each spacecraft, precise pointing to within microradians, and phase-locking the incoming weak beam to a local laser ‚Äì a process called “weak-light phase locking.” Even with this, the received light power will be minuscule, measured in picowatts, demanding exquisitely quiet photoreceivers. Forming and maintaining the stable triangular constellation presents its own orbital mechanics challenge. The three spacecraft orbits must be carefully chosen to keep the arm lengths as equal as possible (varying by less than 1% over the mission) and the triangle angles close to 60 degrees, despite the gravitational tugs of the Sun and planets. This requires constant monitoring and occasional small trajectory correction maneuvers over LISA‚Äôs planned 4+ year operational lifetime. Communication back to Earth for science data downlink and command upload adds another layer of complexity, relying on deep-space communication networks. Finally, the sheer scale and duration demand exceptional reliability; repairs or replacements are impossible once launched. Following the successful technology demonstration by LISA Pathfinder, the European Space Agency (ESA) formally adopted LISA as its third large-class (L3) mission within the Cosmic Vision program, with NASA providing significant contributions, particularly to the laser and telescope systems. After decades of concept studies and technology maturation, LISA is now scheduled for launch in the mid-2030s, poised to become humanity‚Äôs first gravitational wave observatory in space, forever changing our perception of the massive, the ancient, and the warped fabric of the cosmos.

The serene, vast laboratory of space, free from Earth‚Äôs incessant tremors, thus offers the only vantage point from which to perceive the slow, majestic undulations generated by the universe’s most massive objects. As LISA prepares to extend our hearing into the millihertz band, another technique, operating on scales even larger than our Solar System and attuned to frequencies measured in nanohertz, has already begun to listen to the faint, persistent hum of supermassive black holes orbiting each other across the universe. This leads us to the remarkable world of Pulsar Timing Arrays, where the galaxy’s most precise natural clocks become our detectors.

Listening to the Universe’s Hum: Pulsar Timing Arrays

While LISA prepares to extend our gravitational hearing into the millihertz band, unveiling the slow dances of supermassive black holes and galactic binaries, an even more patient and expansive form of listening has already commenced, attuned to frequencies measured in nanohertz. This technique operates not with lasers and vacuum chambers, but by harnessing the galaxy‚Äôs most precise natural timekeepers: millisecond pulsars. Unlike the fleeting chirps or steady hums detectable by ground-based interferometers or LISA, Pulsar Timing Arrays (PTAs) listen for a persistent, universe-spanning background rumble ‚Äì a collective hum generated by the vast population of orbiting supermassive black holes throughout cosmic history. This approach transforms our galaxy into a detector of staggering scale, using the rhythmic pulses of dead stars as probes to sense the subtlest stretching and squeezing of spacetime itself over decades.

Cosmic Clocks: Millisecond Pulsars
The foundation of this galactic-scale observatory lies in the remarkable properties of millisecond pulsars. These celestial metronomes are neutron stars ‚Äì the ultra-dense, city-sized remnants of supernova explosions ‚Äì spinning hundreds of times per second and emitting intense, focused beams of radio waves from their magnetic poles. Due to the misalignment of their magnetic and rotational axes, these beams sweep across Earth like a cosmic lighthouse, resulting in extraordinarily regular pulses of radio emission detectable with large radio telescopes. The key to their utility as gravitational wave detectors is their rotational stability. Millisecond pulsars, having been “spun up” to incredible speeds by accreting matter from a companion star in a previous evolutionary phase, possess rotational periods that rival the precision of the best atomic clocks on Earth. Their stability arises from their immense moment of inertia; their solid crusts, composed of neutron-rich material under incredible pressure, resist changes in spin rate. Tiny irregularities, known as timing noise or “glitches,” do occur, but for the most stable specimens, the predictability of their pulse arrival times (ToAs) over decades is phenomenal, with timing residuals ‚Äì the difference between predicted and actual arrival times ‚Äì often measured in tens to hundreds of nanoseconds over years. The discovery of the first millisecond pulsar, PSR B1937+21, by Donald Backer and colleagues in 1982 using the Arecibo Observatory, opened this new frontier. Since then, dedicated surveys, particularly using powerful facilities like the Green Bank Telescope (GBT), Arecibo (until its collapse), Parkes, and the Low-Frequency Array (LOFAR), have identified over 400 millisecond pulsars. Projects like the North American Nanohertz Observatory for Gravitational Waves (NANOGrav), the European Pulsar Timing Array (EPTA), the Parkes Pulsar Timing Array (PPTA) in Australia, and the Chinese Pulsar Timing Array (CPTA) meticulously monitor tens of the most stable pulsars across the sky for decades, using large radio telescopes to record their pulses with nanosecond precision, building exquisite timing models that account for every known influence: the pulsar’s spin-down, its motion through space relative to Earth, the dispersion of radio waves by interstellar electrons, and even the subtle gravitational effects of planets in our own Solar System. What remains in the timing residuals after accounting for all these factors could hold the signature of passing gravitational waves.

The Principle of a Galactic-Scale Detector
The ingenious concept underlying PTAs leverages the galaxy itself as an interferometer with baselines spanning thousands of light-years. Imagine a gravitational wave ‚Äì a ripple in spacetime ‚Äì passing through our region of the Milky Way. As it propagates, it periodically stretches and compresses the very fabric of space between Earth and each pulsar in the array. This affects the apparent rotational period of the pulsar as seen from Earth. When space between Earth and the pulsar is stretched by the wave, the light travel time increases slightly, causing the next pulse to arrive later than expected. Conversely, when space is compressed, the travel time decreases, and the pulse arrives early. Crucially, the pattern of these timing deviations (residuals) is not random across the pulsar array. It depends on the relative positions of Earth, the pulsar, and the direction of the gravitational wave source (or, for a background, the direction of propagation of each wave component). For a single gravitational wave passing by, pulsars located in specific quadrants of the sky relative to the wave’s propagation direction would exhibit correlated timing residuals ‚Äì some would be early, some late, in a specific geometric pattern. However, the primary target for PTAs is not individual wave sources (though that may eventually be possible), but a stochastic gravitational wave background (GWB) ‚Äì a superposition of countless unresolved gravitational waves, primarily from the cosmic population of supermassive black hole binaries (SMBHBs) inspiraling throughout the universe over billions of years. This background creates a persistent, correlated signal in the pulsar timing residuals. The smoking gun for a gravitational wave origin is a specific angular correlation in the timing deviations between pairs of pulsars, predicted in 1983 by physicists Ron Hellings and George Downs. The Hellings-Downs curve describes how the expected correlation between the timing residuals of any two pulsars depends solely on the angular separation between them on the sky. Pulsars very close together should show highly correlated residuals, while those separated by 90 degrees should be anti-correlated, and those at 180 degrees apart should be correlated again. This unique quadrupolar signature is distinct from any other noise process (like irregularities in individual pulsars or errors in Solar System models) and provides unambiguous evidence for a gravitational wave background. The sensitivity of PTAs peaks in the nanohertz frequency range (periods of years to decades), corresponding perfectly to the orbital periods of SMBHBs with masses of hundreds of millions to tens of billions of solar masses as they inspiral over millions of years before merger.

The individual collaborations, analyzing their independent datasets, consistently found a strong, common-spectrum process: a red noise signal (stronger at lower frequencies) with similar amplitude and spectral characteristics across all the pulsars in their arrays. The Data Analysis Challenge

The compelling evidence for a nanohertz gravitational wave background, emerging from decades of patient pulsar timing as described in the previous section, underscores a fundamental truth of gravitational wave astronomy: the raw signal captured by any detector, whether terrestrial interferometer, space-based constellation, or galactic pulsar array, is invariably buried beneath an overwhelming cacophony of noise. Extracting the faint whispers of cosmic collisions or the persistent hum of the universe from this noisy data deluge represents one of the most formidable computational challenges in modern science. The triumphant announcements of detections belie the sophisticated, often Herculean, efforts in data analysis required to transform subtle perturbations in light phase or pulsar timing residuals into confident discoveries and precise astrophysical insights. This section delves into the sophisticated computational machinery ‚Äì the algorithms, statistical frameworks, and increasingly, artificial intelligence ‚Äì that sifts through the haystack of noise to find the gravitational wave needles, characterizing their sources and unlocking the secrets they carry.

Needles in a Haystack: Signal Extraction
Imagine listening for the faint call of a specific bird species within the roar of a hurricane; this approximates the challenge faced by gravitational wave data analysts. The characteristic “chirp” of a coalescing binary black hole, lasting fractions of a second, or the months-long sinusoidal drift from a continuous wave source, must be identified within data streams dominated by seismic rumbles, thermal vibrations of atoms, quantum fluctuations of laser light, electronic noise, and a menagerie of non-Gaussian transient disturbances known as “glitches” ‚Äì caused by everything from cosmic ray strikes to power grid fluctuations. The primary weapon against this noise is matched filtering. This technique leverages the fact that the expected waveform for many sources, particularly binary coalescences, can be predicted with high accuracy using Einstein’s equations of General Relativity, solved numerically through large-scale supercomputer simulations. Matched filtering works by systematically comparing the noisy detector data against a vast library of pre-computed theoretical waveforms, or “templates,” each representing a potential signal with specific parameters like component masses and spins. The process involves calculating the correlation ‚Äì essentially, the degree of similarity ‚Äì between each template and segments of the data stream. When a strong correlation occurs, significantly exceeding what random noise fluctuations would typically produce, it flags a potential detection candidate. The effectiveness hinges on the quality of the templates and the sheer scale of the search. For binary black holes, template banks must densely cover the astrophysically plausible parameter space of masses (from a few to hundreds of solar masses) and spins. The search for GW150914, for instance, involved comparing the data against approximately 250,000 distinct templates within the PyCBC analysis pipeline. The resulting signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) ‚Äì the measure of how much the correlated signal stands above the average noise level ‚Äì reached an extraordinary 24 for that first detection, far exceeding the typical threshold of 8 used to trigger alerts. This computationally intensive process, running continuously on global computing grids like the LIGO Data Grid, transforms terabytes of raw, noisy interferometer output into manageable lists of candidate events worthy of deep scrutiny.

Characterizing the Signal: Parameter Estimation
Once a candidate signal is identified through searches like matched filtering, the critical task shifts from mere detection to detailed characterization. Parameter estimation aims to extract the maximum possible astrophysical information encoded within the measured waveform: the masses and spins of the compact objects, the distance to the source, the location on the sky, the orientation of the orbital plane (inclination), and the polarization of the waves. This is fundamentally a problem of Bayesian inference. Given the observed data and a model of the signal (based on General Relativity) and the noise, Bayesian methods calculate the posterior probability distribution for each source parameter ‚Äì essentially, the probability density describing how likely different values of that parameter are, given the data. Sophisticated computational frameworks like LALInference (used by the LIGO-Virgo collaborations) and Bilby (a more flexible, open-source alternative) implement this using algorithms such as Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) or Nested Sampling. These algorithms explore the high-dimensional parameter space, guided by the data, to map out the intricate posterior distributions. For GW150914, this analysis revealed with high confidence that the signal originated from the merger of two black holes with masses around 36 and 29 solar masses, located roughly 1.3 billion light-years away, resulting in a 62-solar-mass remnant. However, parameter estimation also reveals degeneracies and uncertainties. For example, the amplitude of the signal depends on both the source’s distance and its inclination angle relative to our line of sight. A face-on binary (inclination near 0¬∞) appears brighter in gravitational waves than an edge-on one (inclination near 90¬∞) at the same distance. Bayesian analysis naturally quantifies this degeneracy, producing joint posterior distributions showing the correlated uncertainties. Sky localization leverages the time delays and amplitude/phase differences measured by multiple detectors in the global network. The 6.9-millisecond delay between LIGO-Hanford and LIGO-Livingston for GW150914, combined with the signal strength in each detector, constrained the source to a broad arc on the sky spanning hundreds of square degrees. Adding Virgo or KAGRA data dramatically tightens this localization by providing additional timing and amplitude constraints. This process transforms the faint ripple in the data into a quantitative astrophysical portrait, albeit one often painted with probabilistic brushstrokes.

Cosmic Messengers: Multi-Messenger Astronomy

The sophisticated computational machinery described in the previous section ‚Äì the matched filters sifting through noise, the Bayesian inference engines extracting astrophysical parameters, and the machine learning algorithms battling glitches ‚Äì serves a grander purpose beyond cataloging isolated gravitational wave events. Its true power is unleashed when a detection triggers a global alert, mobilizing observatories across the electromagnetic spectrum and beyond to turn their gaze towards the cosmic coordinates whispered by spacetime’s ripple. This coordinated symphony of observation, known as multi-messenger astronomy, transforms gravitational waves from intriguing signals into profound cosmic narratives, revealing the interconnected physics governing the universe’s most violent and transformative events. The landmark event GW170817 stands as the paradigm-shifting proof of concept, a celestial Rosetta Stone that decoded the multi-messenger language of a binary neutron star merger.

GW170817: The Landmark Event
On August 17, 2017, at 12:41:04 UTC, the Advanced LIGO-Hanford and LIGO-Livingston detectors, soon joined by Advanced Virgo, captured a gravitational wave signal unlike any before. Designated GW170817, its duration was significantly longer than typical black hole chirps ‚Äì about 100 seconds ‚Äì and its frequency evolved differently, characteristics immediately suggesting the inspiral of lower-mass objects: neutron stars. Crucially, just 1.7 seconds after the merger time inferred from the gravitational wave signal, NASA’s Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope, and independently ESA’s INTEGRAL satellite, detected a faint, short gamma-ray burst (GRB 170817A) originating from the same region of the sky. This near-simultaneous detection was the first compelling evidence linking binary neutron star (BNS) mergers to the enigmatic short gamma-ray bursts theorized decades earlier. The power of the global detector network became immediately apparent. Combining the time delays and signal amplitudes measured by LIGO Hanford, LIGO Livingston, and Virgo, automated analysis pipelines rapidly constrained the source location to an elliptical region of about 30 square degrees in the southern sky, overlapping with the Fermi localization. This unprecedented precision, broadcast globally within minutes via the Gamma-ray Coordinates Network (GCN) and later the Transient Astronomy Network (TAN), ignited the most extensive astronomical follow-up campaign in history. Dozens of ground-based telescopes swiveled towards the target region, scanning the error box. Less than 11 hours after the gravitational wave detection, the 1-meter Swope Telescope at Las Campanas Observatory in Chile, part of the Swope Supernova Survey led by Maria Drout, identified a new, bright optical source in the lenticular galaxy NGC 4993, approximately 130 million light-years away. This was the kilonova, named AT 2017gfo. Within hours and days, observatories spanning ultraviolet (Hubble Space Telescope), optical (VLT, Gemini, DECam), infrared (Spitzer), and radio (VLA, ALMA) wavelengths confirmed the source and tracked its rapidly evolving glow. The detection of GW170817, GRB 170817A, and AT 2017gfo marked the first time a cosmic event had been observed simultaneously through gravitational waves and light across multiple wavelengths ‚Äì a triumph validating decades of theoretical prediction and technological coordination.

Scientific Treasures Unlocked
GW170817 was not merely a first; it was an overflowing treasure chest of astrophysical discovery. The near-coincident gamma-ray burst provided definitive confirmation that BNS mergers are the progenitors of at least a significant fraction of short GRBs. The short delay between the gravitational wave merger signal and the gamma-ray flash tightly constrained models of GRB jet formation and propagation, suggesting a structured jet viewed slightly off-axis, explaining the burst’s relative faintness despite its proximity. The kilonova itself became the primary revelation. Its rapid rise (peaking within a day) and rapid decline, coupled with a dramatic color evolution from blue to deep red over days, matched predictions for radioactive decay heating from newly synthesized heavy elements. Spectroscopic analysis revealed unmistakable signatures of lanthanides ‚Äì heavy, neutron-rich elements like cesium, tellurium, and likely gold and platinum ‚Äì forged in the neutron-rich debris ejected during the merger via the rapid neutron capture process (r-process). This observation provided the first direct, observational proof that BNS mergers are major cosmic foundries, responsible for producing a substantial fraction of the universe’s heaviest elements, from the gold in jewelry to the uranium in reactors. The event also delivered a novel measurement of the Hubble constant, the rate of cosmic expansion. Gravitational waves provide a direct, calibration-free measurement of the luminosity distance to the source (in this case, ~130 million light-years). Traditional methods measure the Hubble constant by comparing redshifts (velocity) to distance indicators like Cepheid variable stars or Type Ia supernovae. By combining the gravitational wave distance with the host galaxy NGC 4993’s redshift measured electromagnetically, astronomers obtained an independent value for H‚ÇÄ. While the initial uncertainty was relatively large, this “standard siren” method (so-called because gravitational waves provide a direct distance siren call) is free from the calibration uncertainties plaguing traditional cosmic distance ladders and promises increasing precision with future BNS detections, potentially resolving the current tension between different measurements of H‚ÇÄ. Finally, the gravitational waveform itself encoded crucial information about the neutron stars’ internal structure. The final stages of the inspiral are sensitive to tidal deformability ‚Äì how much each neutron star distorts under its companion’s gravitational pull. This deformability depends critically on the equation of state (EoS) of ultra-dense nuclear matter. Analysis of GW170817 ruled out the stiffest EoS models, favoring equations of state that predict neutron star radii around 11-13 kilometers, providing unique constraints on physics at densities exceeding that of an atomic nucleus. Future detections, especially with the enhanced sensitivity of detectors like KAGRA probing tidal effects more deeply, will further refine our understanding of matter under extreme conditions.
