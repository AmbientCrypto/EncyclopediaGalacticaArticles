When a system at equilibrium is disturbed, it responds in predictable ways to minimize the effect of the disturbance. This fundamental response pattern manifests across seemingly unrelated domains—from chemical reactions adjusting to added reactants to ecosystems adapting to environmental changes and markets responding to supply shocks.

The triggers for equilibrium shifts are as diverse as the systems themselves. In chemical systems, changes in concentration, temperature, pressure, or the addition of catalysts can initiate shifts. Differential equations and stability analysis form the bedrock of mathematical approaches to understanding equilibrium shifts. These powerful tools allow scientists and mathematicians to describe how systems evolve over time and to classify equilibrium points according to their stability properties. Linear systems, though often simplifications of more complex real-world phenomena, provide essential insights into equilibrium behavior.
