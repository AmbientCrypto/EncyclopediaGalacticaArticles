# Isolation Tank Therapy

## Introduction and Overview of Isolation Tank Therapy

Isolation tank therapy, also known as sensory deprivation or floatation therapy, represents one of the most intriguing intersections of neuroscience, psychology, and wellness practices to emerge in the modern era. At its core, this therapeutic approach involves immersing individuals in a specially designed tank or pod filled with water saturated with Epsom salt (magnesium sulfate), heated precisely to skin temperature (approximately 35.5°C or 95°F), creating an environment that radically reduces external sensory input. The high concentration of salt—typically around 500-600 kilograms per thousand liters of water—creates buoyancy that allows users to float effortlessly, experiencing a sensation of weightlessness that has been described as akin to floating in space or returning to the womb. Scientifically designated as Restricted Environmental Stimulation Technique (REST), isolation tank therapy fundamentally differs from other relaxation and meditation practices by actively minimizing external stimuli rather than simply teaching practitioners to ignore them. The three pillars of this experience—sensory reduction, buoyancy, and relaxation—work synergistically to create a unique physiological and psychological state that has captivated researchers, wellness enthusiasts, and consciousness explorers for decades.

The isolation tank experience begins long before one enters the water. Upon arrival at a float center, clients typically receive a brief orientation, shower to remove oils and lotions that could contaminate the water, and then step into the tank or pod. Once inside, the door is closed, creating a lightproof, soundproof environment where visual and auditory stimuli are virtually eliminated. The water, precisely maintained at skin temperature, creates a thermal neutrality that makes it difficult to distinguish where the body ends and the water begins. As the user lies back, the dense salt solution provides effortless buoyancy, supporting approximately 90% of the body's weight and creating a profound sense of weightlessness. Standard sessions typically last 60 to 90 minutes, though experienced floaters sometimes extend their sessions to two hours or more. During this time, users progress through distinct stages of experience. Initially, the mind remains active, processing thoughts and adjusting to the unusual environment. As the body relaxes, physical tension melts away, and many report a sense of "dropping" into deeper states of consciousness. The latter portion of the session often features theta brainwave activity, associated with deep relaxation, creativity, and meditation. Upon emerging, users commonly report immediate effects including profound relaxation, pain relief, mental clarity, and a heightened sense of wellbeing that can persist for hours or even days afterward. Regular practitioners often establish a floating schedule ranging from weekly sessions for maintenance to more intensive protocols for specific therapeutic goals.

The terminology surrounding isolation tank therapy has evolved significantly since its inception, reflecting broader shifts in scientific understanding and public perception. When first developed in the 1950s, the practice was termed "sensory deprivation," a phrase that carried connotations of punishment and psychological torture rather than therapeutic benefit. This early terminology stemmed from its origins in controversial government-funded research that explored the effects of sensory isolation on human consciousness. As the practice moved from experimental laboratories to therapeutic settings in the 1970s and 1980s, researchers and practitioners began using the more neutral "sensory restriction" and eventually adopted "Restricted Environmental Stimulation Technique" (REST) as the scientific designation. In commercial settings, "floatation therapy" or simply "floating" became the preferred terms, emphasizing the positive aspects of the experience rather than what was being removed. This linguistic evolution paralleled a dramatic shift in public perception, moving from viewing isolation tanks as experimental devices to recognizing them as legitimate therapeutic tools and eventually embracing them as mainstream wellness offerings. The physical manifestations of the practice have also evolved considerably from John C. Lilly's original experimental chambers to today's sleek, user-friendly designs. Modern variations include enclosed pods for those seeking complete sensory reduction, open pools for individuals who may experience claustrophobia, and cabin-style units that provide more space while maintaining the essential elements of the experience. These innovations, combined with thoughtful rebranding that emphasizes relaxation, recovery, and mindfulness rather than deprivation, have made the practice increasingly accessible to a broader audience beyond the researchers, counterculture enthusiasts, and early adopters who initially championed its benefits.

Today, isolation tank therapy has achieved global prevalence, with centers operating on every continent and in countries ranging from the United States and Canada across Europe to Australia, New Zealand, Japan, and increasingly in South America, Africa, and the Middle East. The distribution of float centers remains somewhat uneven, with North America, Western Europe, and Australia/New Zealand boasting the highest concentration of facilities, while other regions are experiencing rapid growth. In the United States alone, the number of commercial float centers has grown from fewer than a dozen in the early 2000s to over 300 by the mid-2010s, with continued expansion into both urban and suburban markets. This growth reflects a broader wellness trend that has seen floatation therapy integrated into spas, gyms, physical therapy practices, and wellness retreats alongside established modalities like massage, meditation, and yoga. Despite this expansion, accessibility remains uneven due to cost considerations, with single sessions typically ranging from $50 to $100 in most markets, making regular practice a significant financial commitment for many. This economic barrier has fueled the emergence of home isolation tanks and DIY options, with companies offering residential units ranging from compact pods to elaborate custom installations. The home float market has grown particularly among dedicated practitioners, athletes, and individuals with specific health conditions who benefit from more frequent access. The floatation industry has also developed professional organizations such as the Float Tank Association and the European Floatation Association, which establish standards, promote research, and support the growing community of practitioners and business owners. Looking forward, industry analysts project continued growth as scientific validation accumulates, public awareness increases, and technological innovations make tanks more affordable and accessible. This global expansion represents a remarkable journey for a practice that began as a scientific curiosity, navigated through periods of controversy and counterculture association, and has emerged as a respected component of the contemporary wellness landscape.

The journey of isolation tank therapy from experimental apparatus to wellness phenomenon offers a fascinating window into the evolving relationship between technology, consciousness, and human wellbeing. As we explore the historical development and origins of this practice in the following section, we will discover how the convergence of scientific curiosity, government interest, counterculture exploration, and entrepreneurial vision shaped what has become one of the most unique and compelling therapeutic modalities of our time. The story of isolation tank therapy is not merely about a physical device or technique, but about humanity's enduring quest to understand consciousness, reduce suffering, and access deeper states of awareness through the strategic reduction of sensory input—a journey that continues to unfold in research centers, wellness facilities, and homes around the world.

## Historical Development and Origins

The fascinating history of isolation tank therapy begins in the scientific laboratories of the 1950s, where a curious neuroscientist's quest to understand consciousness would give birth to one of the most unique therapeutic modalities of our time. John C. Lilly, a physician, psychoanalyst, and neuroscientist working at the National Institute of Mental Health, invented the first isolation tank in 1954, driven by profound questions about the nature of human consciousness and brain function. Lilly, who had previously conducted pioneering research on brain stimulation and neurophysiology, wondered what would happen to the mind when freed from the constant bombardment of external sensory input. His first experimental apparatus was a vertical chamber filled with water, designed to study the effects of sensory deprivation on the brain. Lilly himself became the first human subject, spending hours suspended in the primitive tank while monitoring his own consciousness. These early self-experiments revealed that rather than simply shutting down, the brain remained remarkably active in the absence of external stimuli, generating its own internal experiences that ranged from memories and fantasies to what Lilly described as encounters with deeper aspects of consciousness. His findings, published in scientific journals in the late 1950s, suggested that sensory restriction could be a powerful tool for exploring the mind's inner workings. Lilly's unique background bridging medicine, psychoanalysis, and neuroscience allowed him to approach his research with an interdisciplinary perspective that was ahead of its time, though his later work would take him far beyond the mainstream scientific community.

The 1960s and 1970s saw government and military agencies develop a keen interest in sensory deprivation research, though for purposes far removed from Lilly's original consciousness exploration. The CIA's controversial MKUltra program, which operated from 1953 to 1973, explored sensory deprivation as part of broader research into mind control, interrogation techniques, and psychological operations. Concurrently, Canadian psychologist Donald Hebb conducted experiments at McGill University, funded by both the Canadian military and the CIA, that subjected volunteers to prolonged sensory deprivation with disturbing results. These studies, which often involved placing participants in conditions of severe sensory restriction for extended periods, frequently produced hallucinations, severe anxiety, cognitive impairment, and in some cases lasting psychological disturbances. The U.S. military also investigated sensory deprivation for applications ranging from survival training to developing resistance against enemy interrogation techniques. Meanwhile, NASA studied the effects of sensory isolation to better prepare astronauts for the psychological challenges of space travel, where sensory input would be radically different from terrestrial experience. These government-sponsored applications cast a shadow over sensory deprivation, creating a public perception of it as potentially harmful or even torturous. The stigma was further cemented when Senate hearings in the 1970s, particularly the Church Committee investigations into intelligence abuses, exposed the extent of these controversial programs. Classified documents declassified in subsequent years revealed the depth of government interest in sensory deprivation as a tool for psychological manipulation, a legacy that would take decades to overcome.

The 1970s and 1980s witnessed a remarkable cultural shift as isolation tanks moved from government laboratories to counterculture circles, embraced by a generation exploring consciousness expansion and alternative states of awareness. John Lilly himself had evolved beyond his mainstream scientific roots, becoming interested in psychedelic drugs and forming connections with prominent figures in the consciousness exploration movement including Timothy Leary and Ram Dass. Lilly's books, particularly "The Center of the Cyclone" (1972) and "The Scientist" (1978), brought his experiences with isolation tanks to a broader audience, describing profound encounters with what he termed "inner self" and "cosmic consciousness." These publications, blending scientific inquiry with personal exploration, helped catalyze a broader interest in the mechanisms underlying the profound experiences reported during isolation tank sessions. As the practice transitioned from counterculture curiosity to mainstream wellness offering, researchers increasingly turned their attention to understanding the scientific principles that make floatation therapy so effective. This growing body of research has revealed that the seemingly simple act of floating in a sensory-reduced environment triggers a complex cascade of physiological and psychological responses that work together to produce the wide-ranging benefits attributed to isolation tank therapy.

Sensory restriction theory provides the foundational framework for understanding how isolation tanks affect human consciousness and physiology. The human brain has evolved to continuously process and interpret sensory information from the environment, with an estimated eleven million sensory bits per second entering the nervous system through various channels. In an isolation tank, this constant stream of information is dramatically reduced, creating what neuroscientists call a "sensory-restricted environment." Visual input is eliminated in the lightproof enclosure, auditory stimuli are minimized by the soundproof design and muffling properties of water, tactile sensations are reduced by the uniform water temperature and pressure, and proprioceptive input—the sense of body position and movement—is diminished through weightless buoyancy. This radical reduction of external stimuli creates a state that researchers have termed "sensory hunger," wherein the brain, deprived of its usual input, begins to turn inward. The brain's response to this deprivation varies significantly among individuals, influenced by factors such as personality traits, prior meditation experience, and even genetic predispositions to sensory processing styles. Some people quickly adapt to the reduced input and enter deeply relaxed states, while others may initially experience heightened awareness of internal sensations or even mild anxiety before adapting to the unique environment. This individual variability has been documented in numerous studies, including research by Dr. Arreed Barabasz at Washington State University, who found that approximately 15-20% of first-time floaters report some degree of initial discomfort before settling into the experience. The neurological environment created by sensory restriction is unique because it allows the brain to enter states of consciousness rarely accessible in everyday life, where internal processes can unfold without the constant modulation required by external stimuli.

The neurophysiological responses to floatation therapy represent some of the most compelling scientific evidence for its effectiveness. Electroencephalography (EEG) studies conducted on floaters have consistently shown a shift in brain wave patterns from the beta waves associated with active, alert consciousness to alpha and theta waves linked with relaxation, meditation, and creativity. A pioneering study by Dr. Peter Suedfeld at the University of British Columbia found that after just one hour of flotation, participants showed significant increases in theta wave activity, particularly in the frontal regions of the brain associated with executive function and creativity. Furthermore, research has demonstrated a phenomenon known as cortical synchronization, where brain activity becomes more coherent across different regions, suggesting improved communication between the left and right hemispheres. This hemispheric coherence has been associated with enhanced cognitive performance, creativity, and emotional regulation. The concept of neuroplasticity—the brain's ability to reorganize itself by forming new neural connections—is particularly relevant to floatation therapy, as regular sessions appear to strengthen neural pathways associated with relaxation and stress reduction while weakening those linked to anxiety and reactivity. Neurochemical studies have revealed that floatation triggers the release of several key neurotransmitters, including endorphins (the body's natural painkillers), dopamine (associated with reward and motivation), and serotonin (linked to mood regulation and wellbeing). Research by Dr. Justin Feinstein at the Laureate Institute for Brain Research has shown that even a single float session can lead to measurable decreases in the activity of the default mode network—a brain network associated with self-referential thinking and rumination that is often overactive in conditions like depression and anxiety. These neurophysiological changes help explain why floatation therapy can produce such rapid and profound effects on both mental and physical wellbeing.

The relaxation response and stress reduction mechanisms activated during floatation therapy represent another crucial dimension of its scientific foundation. When a person enters an isolation tank, the parasympathetic nervous system—the branch responsible for the "rest and digest" functions—becomes dominant, while the sympathetic nervous system associated with "fight or flight" responses recedes. This shift triggers a cascade of physiological changes that collectively constitute the relaxation response, a term coined by Dr. Herbert Benson of Harvard Medical School. Numerous studies have documented significant reductions in cortisol, the primary stress hormone, following floatation sessions. Research conducted at the Karolinska Institute in Sweden found that cortisol levels decreased by an average of 21.6% after a single float session, with even greater reductions observed in regular floaters. Beyond hormonal changes, floatation produces measurable alterations in physiological markers of stress: heart rate typically decreases by 10-15 beats per minute, blood pressure drops by approximately 10-15%, and muscle tension throughout the body diminishes significantly. The relationship between sensory restriction and stress recovery can be understood through the concept of "allostatic load"—the cumulative wear and tear on the body from chronic stress. Floatation therapy appears to reduce this load by providing a profound recovery period where the stress response system can reset and rebalance. Modern isolation tanks come in several distinct configurations, each with specific advantages for different user preferences and applications. The most common design is the enclosed pod or capsule, typically measuring approximately 2.4 meters in length and 1.5 meters in width, featuring a hinged or sliding door that can be opened or closed according to user preference. These enclosed designs offer the most complete sensory reduction by eliminating light and sound, though they can induce claustrophobia in approximately 5-10% of first-time users according to industry surveys. Open cabin designs have gained popularity in recent years, featuring higher ceilings of approximately 1.8 meters and a more spacious feel while still maintaining the essential elements of the floatation experience. At the larger end of the spectrum, room-sized float pools or "float rooms" provide the most spacious environment, with dimensions up to 2.5 meters by 2.5 meters, allowing for greater freedom of movement and making them particularly suitable for therapeutic applications involving physical therapy or for individuals with mobility limitations. The evolution from Lilly's original vertical chamber to these contemporary designs reflects decades of user feedback and engineering refinements aimed at balancing the competing demands of complete sensory reduction with user comfort and accessibility. The water composition within isolation tanks represents one of the most critical technical aspects of the floatation experience, requiring precise chemical formulation and diligent maintenance. The solution consists of water saturated with Epsom salt (magnesium sulfate heptahydrate), typically at concentrations of 500-600 kilograms per thousand liters of water, creating a specific gravity of approximately 1.25-1.3. This density allows even the densest human body to float effortlessly with approximately 90% of its weight supported by the buoyant force. The choice of Epsom salt over other salts is deliberate: magnesium sulfate offers several advantages including high solubility in water, skin-softening properties, and potential therapeutic benefits through transdermal absorption during the float session. Maintaining this solution requires sophisticated filtration and sterilization systems that operate continuously between sessions. Most commercial tanks employ multi-stage filtration systems beginning with surface skimmers to remove hair and larger particles, followed by cartridge filters (typically 5-10 microns) to remove finer particulates, and finally ultraviolet (UV) light systems and ozone generators to sterilize the water without introducing chemicals that might irritate skin or interfere with the sensory reduction experience. The pH of the solution must be carefully maintained between 7.2 and 7.6, slightly alkaline to prevent corrosion of tank components while remaining comfortable for skin contact. Operators regularly test water chemistry and add small amounts of hydrogen peroxide or other approved sanitizers as needed, though the high salt concentration itself inhibits most bacterial growth. Environmental considerations for salt water disposal have become increasingly important as the industry grows, with many commercial facilities implementing recirculation systems or working with specialized waste management companies to properly handle the magnesium sulfate solution when it eventually requires replacement. The water temperature must be maintained with extraordinary precision at approximately 35.5°C (95°F), which matches the temperature of human skin and creates the sensation of thermal neutrality where the boundary between body and water becomes perceptually blurred. This temperature regulation is achieved through sophisticated heating systems with multiple redundant sensors and controllers to prevent dangerous overheating, a critical safety feature given that users are typically alone and unsupervised during sessions.

Environmental control systems in isolation tanks represent a marvel of engineering precision, designed to create and maintain the delicate balance of conditions necessary for optimal sensory reduction. Temperature regulation systems typically employ multiple heating elements with digital controllers that can maintain water temperature within 0.1°C of the target, while separate systems regulate air temperature to match that of the water, preventing uncomfortable thermal gradients at the water-air interface. Soundproofing presents particular engineering challenges, as modern isolation tanks must block external noise while also minimizing internal sounds such as water movement, breathing, or the user's own heartbeat. Advanced tanks employ multiple layers of sound-dampening materials including mass-loaded vinyl, acoustic foam, and specialized composite barriers that can reduce external noise by up to 90 decibels. Some manufacturers have even developed active noise cancellation systems that generate sound waves to counteract any residual noise that penetrates the physical barriers. Light control is equally crucial, with most tanks featuring lightproof construction and interior lighting systems that can be adjusted from complete darkness to various levels of illumination according to user preference. Ventilation systems must maintain adequate air quality and prevent the buildup of carbon dioxide without introducing noise or compromising the sensory reduction environment. Sophisticated tanks employ whisper-quiet ventilation systems with HEPA filtration, carefully positioned air inlets and outlets to create minimal air movement that users might perceive, and carbon dioxide sensors that adjust ventilation rates as needed. The challenge of creating a truly sensory-reduced environment extends to eliminating subtle environmental factors that most people never consciously notice, such as electromagnetic fields or faint vibrations from building systems. Some research-grade tanks incorporate Faraday cages to block electromagnetic radiation, while commercial installations often include vibration-damping mounts to isolate the tank from building movements. The integration of these various environmental control systems requires sophisticated engineering to ensure they work harmoniously without introducing new sensory inputs, representing one of the most complex aspects of isolation tank design.

The construction materials and engineering principles underlying modern isolation tanks reflect both practical considerations and aesthetic sensibilities that have evolved significantly over decades of development. Early tanks were constructed from basic materials like wood and metal, but contemporary units typically employ advanced composites chosen for their durability, thermal properties, and aesthetic qualities. Fiberglass remains the most common material for tank shells due to its excellent strength-to-weight ratio, thermal insulation properties, and ease of forming into the ergonomic shapes that enhance user comfort. Higher-end tanks often feature acrylic interiors that provide a smoother, more pleasant surface contact with skin, while some manufacturers have begun using medical-grade stainless steel for research applications requiring exceptional cleanliness and durability. The structural engineering of isolation tanks must account for several unique challenges: supporting the tremendous weight of the water-salt solution (a medium-sized tank holds approximately 1,000 liters of water plus 500-600 kilograms of salt, totaling over 1.5 tons), preventing leaks through carefully designed seams and seals, and maintaining structural integrity over years of use in potentially humid environments. The floor beneath a commercial float tank must typically be reinforced to support weights exceeding 2,500 kilograms per square meter, a significant structural consideration for facilities adding floatation services to existing buildings. The musculoskeletal system undergoes perhaps the most immediately noticeable changes during isolation tank therapy, responding dramatically to the near-complete elimination of gravitational stress through buoyancy. In terrestrial environments, the human body constantly engages muscles and compresses joints to maintain posture and movement against gravity's relentless pull. Within the isolation tank, however, the dense Epsom salt solution supports approximately 90% of the body's weight, creating a state of weightless suspension that allows muscles and joints to decompress fully. This combination makes isolation tank therapy particularly effective for conditions characterized by muscle tension and chronic pain, where the therapeutic triad of weightlessness, sensory reduction, and magnesium absorption works synergistically to interrupt pain cycles and promote healing.

A compelling 2020 study led by Dr. Justin Feinstein at the Laureate Institute for Brain Research used advanced ultrasound imaging to visualize changes in blood flow during floatation, revealing a 15-20% increase in cerebral blood flow accompanied by significant decreases in peripheral vascular resistance. These circulatory changes have important implications for cardiovascular health, particularly for individuals with hypertension or those at risk for cardiovascular disease. The weightless environment also eliminates the circulatory challenges associated with upright posture, where the cardiovascular system must work harder to pump blood upward against gravity. ## Psychological and Neurological Effects

The cumulative endocrine effects observed with regular floatation practice naturally extend into profound psychological and neurological transformations, revealing the intricate connections between bodily physiology and mental experience. As the body's stress response systems recalibrate through repeated sessions, the mind undergoes equally significant shifts, accessing states of consciousness and cognitive processes rarely encountered in ordinary waking life. These psychological and neurological effects represent perhaps the most fascinating dimension of isolation tank therapy, demonstrating how strategic sensory reduction can unlock dormant capacities of the human mind while simultaneously providing powerful therapeutic interventions for mental health conditions. The exploration of these inner landscapes has captivated researchers and practitioners alike, revealing that the isolation tank serves not merely as a relaxation device but as a sophisticated tool for consciousness exploration and psychological healing.

The range of conscious states experienced during floatation therapy spans a remarkable spectrum, progressing through distinct phases as sensory input diminishes and the mind turns inward. Upon entering the tank, most individuals initially maintain normal waking consciousness, actively processing thoughts and adjusting to the unusual environment. This phase typically lasts 10-20 minutes before the mind begins to transition into altered states of awareness as the body fully relaxes and external stimuli recede. During this intermediate period, many floaters report experiencing hypnagogia—the dreamlike state between waking and sleeping characterized by flowing imagery, abstract thoughts, and occasional hallucinations. Research by Dr. Arreed Barabasz at Washington State University has documented that approximately 40% of first-time floaters experience vivid hypnagogic imagery, often described as geometric patterns, scenes from memory, or symbolic narratives that unfold without conscious direction. As the session progresses deeper, consciousness can shift into theta-dominated states associated with deep meditation, where boundaries between self and environment may begin to dissolve. In this state, many floaters report experiences that transcend ordinary perception, including sensations of boundless expansion, unity with the universe, encounters with archetypal imagery, or what Dr. Stanislav Grof terms "holotropic experiences"—states that access deep levels of the psyche. These profound consciousness shifts have been systematically studied by researchers like Dr. Justin Feinstein at the Laureate Institute for Brain Research, who using functional MRI has observed dramatic decreases in activity within the default mode network during floatation, correlating with self-reported experiences of self-transcendence and ego dissolution. The neurological mechanisms underlying these consciousness changes involve complex interactions between reduced sensory input, altered neurotransmitter balance, and shifts in brain wave patterns. Particularly noteworthy is the synchronization of theta waves across multiple brain regions during deep float states, creating a neuroelectric environment similar to that observed in experienced meditators during peak experiences. This theta synchronization appears to facilitate access to subconscious material while simultaneously maintaining a witness-like awareness that allows for observation without judgment—a unique combination that distinguishes floatation-induced states from ordinary dreaming or drug-induced altered states.

Beyond consciousness exploration, isolation tank therapy produces significant cognitive effects that enhance mental performance across multiple domains. The reduction of external stimuli creates what cognitive scientists call a "low-load" environment, freeing attentional resources that are typically consumed by processing sensory information. This attentional liberation enables enhanced creativity and problem-solving abilities, as documented in several controlled studies. A particularly compelling experiment conducted at the University of British Columbia found that participants who completed a 90-minute float session before attempting creative problem-solving tasks generated 30% more original solutions compared to control groups, with improvements particularly pronounced in tasks requiring divergent thinking. The cognitive benefits extend to memory consolidation and learning enhancement as well. Research published in the journal *Learning and Memory* demonstrated that floating after learning new information improved recall by approximately 25% compared to participants who engaged in quiet rest, suggesting that the theta brainwave state during floatation facilitates the transfer of information from short-term to long-term memory storage. The mechanisms underlying these cognitive enhancements involve several complementary processes: the reduction of cognitive load allows previously occupied neural networks to reorganize; the theta state promotes neuroplastic changes that strengthen memory traces; and the relaxation response reduces stress hormones that otherwise interfere with optimal cognitive functioning. Professional athletes and performers have capitalized on these cognitive effects, with Olympic teams and performing arts schools incorporating floatation into training regimens specifically to enhance skill acquisition and mental rehearsal capabilities. A fascinating case study involves a professional musician who reported solving a complex composition problem that had stumped him for months during a single float session, attributing the breakthrough to the unrestricted flow of musical ideas that emerged without the usual critical filtering. These cognitive enhancements represent perhaps the most practical application of floatation therapy for individuals seeking to optimize mental performance in professional, academic, or creative endeavors.

For anxiety disorders, floatation offers a unique therapeutic approach by simultaneously addressing both physiological arousal and cognitive patterns that maintain anxiety.
